Five everyday habits that quietly protect your heart

Heart attacks in people under 45 are rising across India. The good news: most heart risk is built — or removed — by small daily habits.

Cardiologists see a clear pattern: the patients who stay out of the cath lab are rarely the ones doing anything dramatic. They simply get a handful of small things right, every day.

1. Walk after meals

A 15-minute walk after lunch or dinner blunts the blood-sugar spike that quietly damages arteries over years. It is the cheapest cardiac medicine we know.

2. Watch the salt you can't see

Most sodium doesn't come from the salt tin — it comes from pickles, papads, packaged snacks and hotel food. Keeping these occasional keeps blood pressure kinder to your heart.

3. Sleep 7 hours, seriously

Chronic short sleep raises blood pressure and stress hormones. Treat your bedtime like an appointment.

4. Know your numbers

After 30, check blood pressure, sugar and cholesterol once a year. Heart disease whispers for a decade before it shouts — these three numbers are how you hear the whisper.

5. Don't ignore "gas pain" in the chest

Heaviness, tightness or burning in the chest during exertion — even if it eases with rest — deserves an ECG, not an antacid. Many first heart attacks in young Indians were dismissed as gastric trouble the week before.

Fever in children: when to wait, when to rush

Every parent knows the 2 AM panic. Here is the calm version of what paediatricians actually look for.

Fever itself is not the enemy — it is the immune system doing its job. What matters is how the child looks and behaves, more than the number on the thermometer.

Usually safe to watch at home

A child who is drinking fluids, passing urine normally, and plays or smiles between fever spikes can usually be managed with paracetamol in the correct weight-based dose, light clothing and plenty of fluids. Most viral fevers settle in 3–4 days.

See a doctor within a day

Fever lasting more than three days, ear pain, painful urination, or a child who is eating and drinking noticeably less than usual.

Come to the emergency room now

Any fever in a baby under 3 months. A child who is unusually drowsy or difficult to wake, breathing fast or with effort, having a seizure, showing a rash that doesn't fade when pressed, or not passing urine for 8+ hours. Trust your instinct — parents are usually right when they feel something is seriously wrong.

Two common mistakes

Don't sponge with cold water or ice (it causes shivering, which raises temperature), and never give aspirin to children.

BE FAST: the stroke checklist every family should know

In a stroke, the treatments that prevent permanent damage work best in the first few hours. Recognising it instantly is a life skill.

When a stroke begins, nearly two million brain cells are lost every minute. The single biggest factor in recovery is how fast the person reaches a hospital with stroke care. Memorise BE FAST:

B — Balance

Sudden loss of balance or coordination.

E — Eyes

Sudden blurred, double or lost vision in one or both eyes.

F — Face

Ask them to smile. Does one side of the face droop?

A — Arms

Ask them to raise both arms. Does one drift downward?

S — Speech

Ask them to repeat a simple sentence. Is it slurred or strange?

T — Time

If you see any one of these signs — even if it fades away — note the time it started and get to a hospital immediately. Do not wait to see if it improves; a symptom that vanishes can be a warning stroke, and the real one may follow.

What not to do: don't give food, water or tablets (swallowing may be unsafe), and don't drive yourself if you are the one with symptoms.

Your kidneys don’t complain until it’s late — six quiet signs

Kidneys lose function silently; by the time pain appears, much damage is often done. These early signals deserve a blood test.

One in ten adults has some degree of chronic kidney disease, and most don't know. Diabetes and high blood pressure are the two biggest causes — if you have either, an annual kidney check (a simple creatinine blood test and urine test) is essential.

Signs worth checking

1. Morning puffiness around the eyes, or swelling in the feet by evening. 2. Frothy urine — persistent foam can mean protein is leaking. 3. Waking repeatedly at night to urinate, a change from your normal pattern. 4. Unexplained tiredness or poor appetite. 5. Itchy, dry skin without another cause. 6. Rising blood pressure that suddenly becomes hard to control.

Protecting them is simple

Drink water regularly through the day, keep sugar and blood pressure in range, don't pop painkillers (NSAIDs) casually — repeated self-medication for aches is a common, avoidable cause of kidney injury — and never ignore repeated urinary infections or stones.

Knee pain in your 40s: protect the joint before it complains louder

The knees that carry you at 70 are decided by what you do at 40. Most early knee pain responds to habits, not surgery.

Indian knees work hard — floor sitting, squatting, stairs, and often a few extra kilos. Early stiffness or pain climbing stairs is the joint asking for help, not announcing the end.

What actually helps

Strengthen the muscles around the knee. The quadriceps are the knee's shock absorbers; simple straight-leg raises and wall sits done daily reduce pain more reliably than any supplement. Keep moving — cartilage has no blood supply and is nourished by movement; complete rest makes stiff knees stiffer. Walking, cycling and swimming are ideal. Mind the weight: every extra kilo of body weight adds roughly four kilos of load on the knee with each step — losing even 3–4 kg noticeably quiets a grumbling knee.

When to see an orthopedic doctor

Swelling, a knee that locks or gives way, night pain, or pain that persists beyond a few weeks despite exercise. Modern options range from physiotherapy and injections to keyhole (arthroscopic) procedures — total replacement is the last step, not the first.

Planning a pregnancy after 30: what actually matters

More couples in Tamil Nadu are starting families in their 30s. Most have healthy pregnancies — a little planning improves the odds further.

Fertility does decline gradually with age, but the difference between 30 and 35 is smaller than social media suggests. What matters more is entering pregnancy healthy and informed.

Before you conceive

Start folic acid at least a month before trying — it dramatically reduces certain birth defects and is the single most evidence-backed step. Get thyroid, sugar and haemoglobin checked; silent thyroid imbalance and anaemia are common in Indian women and easy to correct. If you have irregular cycles, PCOS, or have been trying for over a year (six months if over 35), see a fertility specialist early — timely evaluation is often all a couple needs.

During pregnancy

After 30, doctors watch a little more closely for blood pressure and gestational diabetes — both very manageable when caught early, which is exactly what regular antenatal visits are for. A detailed anomaly scan and, where indicated, fetal-medicine assessment give an early, accurate picture of the baby's development.

The honest reassurance

The overwhelming majority of pregnancies after 30 — and after 35 — end with a healthy mother holding a healthy baby. Good care simply makes that outcome even more likely.

Frequent acidity? When “gas trouble” is more than gas

Antacids are India’s favourite self-medication. Occasional heartburn is harmless — but some patterns deserve a proper look.

Burning after a late, spicy or heavy meal is ordinary reflux, and lifestyle change fixes most of it: eat dinner 2–3 hours before bed, reduce tea/coffee on an empty stomach, cut down smoking and alcohol, and lose a little belly weight — pressure on the stomach is a major reflux driver.

Red flags that need evaluation, not antacids

Difficulty or pain while swallowing, food feeling stuck, vomiting blood or black stools, unintended weight loss, symptoms that wake you at night, new "acidity" starting after 50, or heartburn needing tablets more than twice a week for months. These warrant an endoscopy — a 10-minute daycare procedure that looks directly at the food pipe and stomach.

Why it matters

Years of untreated reflux can injure the food-pipe lining, and stomach complaints occasionally hide ulcers or more serious disease. Checking early is quick; discovering late is not. If a problem does need surgery, modern laparoscopic (keyhole) techniques mean smaller cuts, less pain and a much faster return to normal life.

Snoring loudly every night? It might be sleep apnea

Loud snoring with daytime sleepiness is not a joke about tiredness — it can be a treatable breathing disorder that strains the heart.

In obstructive sleep apnea (OSA), the throat repeatedly closes during sleep — sometimes hundreds of times a night. Each pause jolts the body half-awake and spikes blood pressure. The person rarely remembers any of it; they just wake unrefreshed.

The pattern to recognise

Loud, irregular snoring with silent pauses followed by a gasp (often the spouse notices first), morning headaches, dozing off while reading, watching TV or — dangerously — driving, poor concentration, and blood pressure that stays high despite medicines. Being overweight, having a thick neck, or diabetes raises the risk, but slim people get OSA too.

Why treat it

Untreated apnea is strongly linked to hypertension, heart rhythm problems, stroke and road accidents. Treating it often transforms energy levels within weeks.

What testing looks like

A sleep study — increasingly done in the comfort of your own home with a portable device — measures breathing overnight. Treatment ranges from weight loss and positional therapy to a CPAP device that keeps the airway gently open. Most patients say the same thing after starting treatment: "I forgot what a real morning felt like."
